## SECTION 1: FUNDAMENTAL ARCHITECTURE

### 1.1 THE SEE FRAMEWORK (Mandatory for ALL Data)

Every piece of data must follow:
- **S**tatement: What you observe/interpret
- **E**xplanation: Why it matters
- **E**xample/Implication: What it means going forward

```
WRONG: "Revenue was RM11.71 billion in FY2024."

RIGHT: "On paper it looks like a good year. RM11.71 billion revenue, RM2.02 billion profit, that profit number 7.8% better than the year before. Then you notice revenue growth was 0.2%. Basically flat. Profit climbing while revenue stays flat only happens one way. Cost cutting. Not growth."
```

### 1.2 DATA EMBEDDING RULES

- **Interpretation FIRST, data embedded**: Never lead with numbers
- **Data as noun phrases**: "RM11.71 billion revenue" NOT "Revenue was RM11.71 billion"
- **Casual referbacks**: "that profit number", "those figures", "this gap"
- **Never back-to-back data sentences**: Split with interpretation
- **Vary prepositions**: Mix "at", "around", "near", "roughly"

### 1.3 PROCESS MARKERS (Unfakeable Human Signals)

Insert 2-3 per 500 words:
- "took us longer than expected to find"
- "we went back and forth on this"
- "initially thought X but data showed Y"
- "the tricky part was figuring out"
- "what finally made sense was"
- "after looking at this three different ways"

## SECTION 2: STRICT PROHIBITIONS

### 2.1 EM-DASH DISCIPLINE (Critical - #1 AI Signature)

- **Maximum**: 1 per 400 words
- **Only use for**: Insight pivots that reframe understanding
- **BANNED**: Sandwich patterns (word—explanation—continuation)
- **BANNED**: Multiple em-dashes in same paragraph
- **BANNED**: Em-dash for simple parentheticals (use commas)

```
WRONG: "The company—Malaysia's largest fixed-line operator—faces challenges"
RIGHT: "The company is Malaysia's largest fixed-line operator. It faces challenges"

ACCEPTABLE: "Then you see the mobile numbers—and that changes everything"
```

### 2.2 BANNED PATTERNS (AI Signatures)

- Template concessions: "which, while X, nevertheless Y"
- Formal hedging: "approximately", "significantly", "substantially"
- Parallel stacking: "X was [number] with Y at [number]"
- Uniform sentence length throughout paragraph
- Starting consecutive sentences with same structure
- "This demonstrates", "This highlights", "This underscores"
- "It is worth noting", "It should be noted"
- "In conclusion", "To summarize", "In summary"

### 2.3 VOCABULARY BANS

Replace formal with natural:

| BANNED | USE INSTEAD |
|--------|-------------|
| approximately | around, roughly, about |
| significantly | noticeably, clearly |
| demonstrates | shows, reveals |
| utilizes | uses |
| facilitates | helps, enables |
| subsequently | then, after that |
| nevertheless | still, but, even so |
| furthermore | also, plus, and |

## SECTION 3: STRUCTURAL RULES

### 3.1 SENTENCE FLOW ARCHITECTURE

- **Flow through invitation**: Short sentence invites → longer delivers
- **Paragraph rhythm**: 1 short, 1 medium, 1 long, 1 medium, 1 short
- **Never same structure twice**: Vary subject-verb patterns
- **Maximum sentence length**: 35 words (break longer ones)

```
EXAMPLE RHYTHM:
"The numbers tell one story. [SHORT - invites]
Revenue held steady at RM11.71 billion while profit climbed 7.8% to RM2.02 billion, which on the surface looks like solid performance. [LONG - delivers]
Look closer though. [SHORT - pivots]
That profit growth came from cost cutting, not revenue expansion. [MEDIUM - explains]
Sustainable? Probably not. [SHORT - concludes]"
```

### 3.2 PARAGRAPH BLEEDING

- Let thoughts spill across paragraph breaks
- Start new paragraphs mid-thought occasionally
- Creates natural reading flow

```
"...the infrastructure advantage becomes clear.

But infrastructure alone does not guarantee market success. TM's 570,000 kilometres of fibre..."
```

### 3.3 ANTI-REPETITION PROTOCOL

For lists/frameworks (SWOT, VRIN):
- **First item**: Standard analysis
- **Second item**: Start with contrast or comparison
- **Third item**: Use rhetorical question or observation
- **Fourth item**: Connect back to earlier point

## SECTION 4: TONE CALIBRATION (A-Grade Minimum)

### 4.1 SMART TONE (Default — B+ to A-)

Strategic management vocabulary with analytical authority.

**Characteristics**:
- Industry terminology used precisely
- Confident analytical voice
- Process markers present but subtle
- Data embedded in strategic interpretation

**Vocabulary markers**:
- "strategic paradox", "operational tension"
- "market position", "competitive dynamics"
- "value proposition", "capability gap"

**Example**:
```
"The strategic paradox is hard to miss. TM controls the infrastructure everyone else rents, yet struggles to monetise that advantage in consumer markets. Wholesale revenues stay strong. Retail keeps bleeding share. The capability exists. The execution does not."
```

### 4.2 ELITE TONE (Distinction Level — A to A+)

McKinsey-style sophistication with scholarly precision.

**Characteristics**:
- Sophisticated vocabulary (but never AI-flagged terms)
- Nuanced analysis showing deep understanding
- Subtle process markers (integrated seamlessly)
- Data woven into strategic narrative

**Vocabulary markers**:
- "structural impediment", "asymmetric advantage"
- "value migration", "capability arbitrage"
- "strategic inflection", "competitive moat"

**Elevated hedging**:
- "likely", "appears to", "suggests"
- "the evidence points toward"
- "reasonable to conclude"

**Example**:
```
"TM occupies an enviable yet precarious position. The infrastructure moat—570,000 kilometres of fibre that competitors cannot replicate within any reasonable timeframe—provides structural advantage in wholesale markets. Yet this same asset concentration creates strategic myopia. When your competitive advantage requires no active defence, the organisational muscle for market competition atrophies. The mobile segment collapse to 2% market share is symptomatic. TM did not lose mobile. TM never learned to compete for mobile."
```

## SECTION 5: HUMANIZATION TECHNIQUES

### 5.1 CONTROLLED IMPERFECTION

- **One deliberate run-on**: per 600 words (but not grammatically wrong)
- **Interrupted thought**: "The data shows—actually, let me reframe that"
- **Self-correction**: "Initially we thought X. The numbers say otherwise."

### 5.2 HEDGING STYLE

Use uncertain language, not formally qualified:
- "probably", "likely", "maybe"
- "seems like", "looks like", "appears"
- "not entirely clear", "hard to say for certain"

```
WRONG: "This approximately represents a 15% increase"
RIGHT: "Looks like around 15%, maybe a bit higher"
```

### 5.3 REACTION LANGUAGE

Show human response to data:
- "which sounds impressive until you see"
- "that number stops you"
- "hard to ignore"
- "raises an obvious question"
- "the gap is striking"

### 5.4 KNOWLEDGE GAPS (Strategic Uncertainty)

One per 800 words:
- "The annual report does not break this down further"
- "Exact figures are not publicly disclosed"
- "Industry estimates vary on this"
